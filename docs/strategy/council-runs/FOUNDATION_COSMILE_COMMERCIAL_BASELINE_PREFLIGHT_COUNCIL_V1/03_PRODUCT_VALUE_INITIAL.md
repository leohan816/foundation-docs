STRONGEST_FINDING:

The audit is justified as a short antidote to further implementation on an unverified commercial baseline, but it will accelerate customer use only if it is converted from an eighteen-output completeness exercise into a stage-gated product decision exercise. The pinned Preflight establishes a real decision problem: current product heads are Memory-shadow heads, no current commercial baseline artifact exists, source existence is not runtime or commercial readiness, and Paid Beta blockers are not separated from Public Launch blockers in a current reviewed baseline. Those facts make a bounded investigation preferable to guessing the next implementation priority. They do not justify treating every proposed flow, readiness criterion, or Foundation surface as equally necessary before customer learning can resume.

The smallest sufficient form is a one-day commercial triage followed, only if needed, by up to two more days of targeted evidence closure. Day 1 must state a concrete Paid Beta decision hypothesis, identify the smallest customer-facing slice, determine whether Foundation is actually required for that slice, and stop early if current evidence already supports a priority or if no meaningful beta hypothesis can be formed. Days 2–3 should then investigate only uncertainties capable of changing that priority. The three-working-day duration should be a maximum, not a promised amount of work or a completeness target.

The Preflight proves evidence drift more strongly than it proves commercial demand. It contains no verified target-customer commitment, willingness-to-pay signal, current funnel or order evidence, named beta cohort, or operator capacity. A technically excellent baseline can therefore still be the wrong next investment if a concierge/manual customer experiment could answer the next business question sooner, or if there is no sufficiently concrete Paid Beta hypothesis against which technical findings can be prioritized. The proposal should return `HOLD` at the Day 1 gate if it cannot name the customer, the outcome, the transaction or consultation being tested, and the decision the evidence will unlock. This does not require Leo to pre-decide the full product; the audit may formulate two or three bounded beta options. It does require a product decision frame before the audit expands into surface mapping.

MISSING_ALTERNATIVES:

### Steelmanned alternative: one-day decision triage with a conditional two-day extension

Run one read-only day on the exact pins to produce only: (1) a current customer/beta hypothesis or two to three beta options, (2) the smallest customer-facing critical path for each viable option, (3) a high-level evidence/status map for that path, (4) a Foundation dependency decision, and (5) a recommendation to implement, continue investigation, or hold. Continue for at most two more days only for named unknowns that can change the recommended first implementation slice.

This is better than the proposal as written because it can stop once the business decision is supportable; it prevents the list of outputs from becoming the purpose; it forces optional platform work out of the beta path; and it makes “three days” a ceiling. It is insufficient as a universal one-day replacement because the current critical flows and mock/real boundaries are genuinely unverified. If Day 1 reveals two credible priorities whose choice depends on call-path, persistence, failure, or external-owner evidence, the two targeted follow-on days remain justified.

### Customer-learning-first alternative

If no credible paid-beta demand signal or cohort exists, defer the technical audit and design a reversible, no-production customer-learning step such as recruiting a small invited cohort, testing the consultation/order proposition manually, or obtaining explicit willingness-to-pay commitments. This is commercially better when demand uncertainty dominates technical uncertainty. It is insufficient when customer commitment already exists and the immediate question is which current implementation gap prevents serving that cohort safely.

### Answers to the twelve common questions

1. **Is the proposed commercial audit actually necessary?** Yes, conditionally. 2. **Is the three-working-day scope appropriate?** Three days is an appropriate ceiling for targeted reconstruction, not for all proposed outputs at equal depth. Use a Day 1 gate and conditional Days 2–3.

6. **Are Paid Beta and Public Launch boundaries sufficiently separated?** Structurally yes; substantively no. Separate status and blocker labels are present, but no concrete option connects each gate to a cohort, value proposition, operating model, and required slice.
7. **Is the evidence model practical and strict enough?** The levels are strong and appropriately strict for money/order/PII/authority paths. The READY rule needs a feature-slice boundary so that noncritical unknowns do not force completeness.
8. **Is the runtime safety envelope useful without being needlessly restrictive?** Yes. Default static-only review is useful; exact safe local command classes may be allowed only when they can change a decision. Disallowed execution must remain `UNVERIFIED`, not become a reason for extension.
